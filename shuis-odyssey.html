Why · The case for the project

Health communication tells the public what dementia is. Very little teaches them what it feels like.

55M+
people worldwide are currently living with dementia; projected to reach 139 million by 2050. — Alzheimer's Disease International
~75%
of dementia cases globally remain undiagnosed — closer to 90% in low-income countries. — BrightFocus Foundation
3 sec
one new case of dementia is diagnosed somewhere in the world every three seconds. — ADI, World Alzheimer Report

Most health communication about dementia teaches the public what the disease is. Very little teaches the public what the disease feels like. Family caregivers describe a particular, repeated frustration: clinicians and relatives can list symptoms but cannot imagine the inner experience of disorientation, of mistaking a daughter for a mother, of a kitchen suddenly becoming unfamiliar. A growing body of research on serious games for dementia suggests that interactive media can close part of this empathy gap — by letting the well briefly inhabit the perceptual world of the unwell. Shui's Odyssey was built inside that hypothesis.

Shui's Odyssey began as a student research project. Our team wanted to test a question we had not seen answered: when the visual grammar of cinematic montage — a tool film and animation use to compress and re-order memory — is handed to the player as an interactive mechanic, what kind of story can it tell?

Alzheimer's disease became our subject because its symptoms are themselves a form of involuntary montage: vision that cannot resolve, memories that surface out of order, the recent past missing while the distant past remains vivid. We wanted players to feel that grammar from the inside, not be told about it from the outside.

How · Design rationale & process

Mechanic-to-symptom mapping: every interaction operationalizes a clinical reality.

A Health Tech reader cares less about how the game looks than about whether the candidate can think rigorously about translating clinical concepts into interactive form, lead a team across disciplines, and ship.

Clinical reality
People with Alzheimer's frequently experience visual-perceptual difficulties: trouble reading, judging distance, distinguishing colors and contrast, recognizing three-dimensional objects, and in later stages, visual hallucinations.
Design response
Visual illusion mechanic. Every puzzle frame is a hand-painted scene where what the player sees and what is actually there diverge. The player must learn to read the image the way a person with AD must learn to read the world — with effort, doubt, and patience.
Clinical reality
Memory in Alzheimer's is asymmetric: distant memories often remain vivid while recent memories vanish first. Patients can be lucid about a childhood meal yet unable to recall a conversation from minutes ago.
Design response
Memory-fragment assembly mechanic. The player pieces together scattered fragments of Shui's life. The temporal logic is deliberately broken — fragments from sixty years ago are sharp, fragments from yesterday are missing.
Clinical reality
Many people with AD lose the ability to reliably recognize the people closest to them, sometimes mistaking a daughter for a mother or a grandchild for a sibling. The misrecognition is not random; it follows the contour of older, stronger memories overwriting newer ones.
Design response
Narrative misrecognition. The protagonist Shui mistakes her daughter Xiuhua for her own mother, and her grandson for her younger brother. The player initially reads these as fantasy; only later do they realize they are reading a clinical symptom.

The game opens with Shui as a young woman. Without warning, she becomes old, and the player begins searching for a way to make her young again.

Hours of play later, the realization arrives quietly: Shui is not under a spell. She is old, and she has been old for a long time. The sudden aging the player has been trying to undo is the way Alzheimer's disease feels from the inside — a self-image frozen at a much earlier age, colliding with a body and a world that have moved on without permission.

The mechanic is the diagnosis. The player learns the disease by being asked to live one of its central confusions.

Team leadership

As Game Director and Producer, I held the project's center: setting creative direction, driving the production schedule across art, design, and engineering, owning external communication with our publisher 2P Games, and stepping into hands-on art and design work whenever a department needed a second pair of hands. The team operated as a hub-and-spoke, and I was the hub — which meant my measure of success was less my own output than the team's ability to keep moving.

A note on research method

The project did not include formal user interviews with people living with dementia. Several team members drew on direct family experience with the disease, and the design was checked against publicly available patient and caregiver writing. A future iteration of this kind of work — especially in a Health Tech context — would build in structured co-design with patients and caregivers from day one. That gap is part of what draws me toward Health-domain teams now.
